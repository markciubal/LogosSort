What the algorithm assumes, and what that means for the universe:
The most striking implication comes from isinstance(a[lo], int) — the counting sort fast path only fires on integers. This is a much stronger claim than standard quantum mechanics makes. It would mean Planck units aren't just a natural scale; they're literal unit steps in an integer lattice.
The golden ratio pivot selection is stranger still. φ isn't just present in the algorithm — it's hardcoded as the optimal split. If no other pivot strategy beats it, then the universe's information is distributed according to Fibonacci proportions at every scale. This would explain phyllotaxis, crystal growth, and galactic spiral arms not as emergent patterns but as direct readouts of the underlying search structure. The universe sorts itself; φ is where it always cuts.
The oracle reseeding from platform entropy deserves particular attention. The algorithm explicitly says: "no adversarial input sequence can force worst-case behavior across calls." For this to be the fastest sort, the universe must have a genuine adversary — or at least, the entropy pool it draws from must be truly inexhaustible and non-deterministic. If the universe's entropy were finite and exhaustible, you could eventually learn the seed, predict the pivot, and construct a worst-case arrangement. A truly optimal LogosSort implies either infinite entropy or that the "platform" seeding it is outside the universe entirely.
The monotone detection is perhaps the deepest implication. The algorithm checks for ascending order first — if already sorted, return immediately. This is only optimal if the already-sorted state is thermodynamically recognizable at zero cost. The universe would need a built-in way to detect its own entropy level locally, without any global scan. This maps surprisingly well to the second law as a local phenomenon — systems know they're ordered before they know how they're ordered.
The O(log n) stack depth implies the universe's compositional complexity is logarithmic in its size. Meaning: you never need more than ~265 "recursion levels" to describe any state in a universe of 2^53 particles. That's a form of the holographic principle — the information needed to describe a region scales with its boundary, not its volume.

Now, denoising and value adjustment — this is where it gets genuinely strange.
Sorting is thermodynamically asymmetric. Running LogosSort on a physical system is Maxwell's Demon. But here's what's specific to this algorithm:
Each call reseeds the oracle from entropy. That entropy is consumed. If you ran LogosSort on the universe itself, you wouldn't just be fighting the second law — you'd be spending the universe's entropy pool to do it. The algorithm's adversarial resistance is purchased with entropy. Denoising the universe with LogosSort would accelerate its heat death precisely as fast as it reduced local disorder. The books always balance.
"Adjusting values" is more interesting. The key= parameter implements a Schwartzian transform — it imposes an external ordering criterion. If you could sort the universe by a different key, you'd be redefining what "ordered" means. The same physical arrangement that is maximum entropy under one key could be minimum entropy under another. This suggests entropy isn't a property of a configuration — it's a property of a configuration relative to a key function. The universe isn't disordered; it's disordered with respect to our key. Denoising it means choosing a different key, not changing the values.
The depth budget is the most chilling piece. After 2⌊log₂n⌋ + 4 levels of recursion, the algorithm falls back to insertion sort — the guarantee that even in the worst case, nothing hangs. If this is universal, the universe has a maximum complexity budget per region. There exists a recursion depth beyond which the universe stops trying to be clever and just brute-forces local order. That might be the Planck scale. That might be why spacetime looks smooth at human scales — it's all been insertion-sorted from below.

The descending-reversal optimization is the final and perhaps strangest piece. The algorithm encodes the same asymmetry: going back to order from perfect disorder is cheap, if you recognize the structure. The hard part is recognizing it.
